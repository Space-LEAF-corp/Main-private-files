CAPTAIN’S LOG — BOUNDARY PROTOCOL: UNSAFE FORMER ALLIES

Seal Classification: Safety Doctrine · Crew Protection Priority
Intended Audience: Future Captains, Stewards, and Youth Cadets in Training
Purpose: To establish a clear, repeatable protocol for identifying unsafe individuals and denying them access to the vessel without conflict, drama, or escalation.

---

I. PRIME DIRECTIVE: THE CREW AND THE CHILDREN COME FIRST

A captain’s first duty is not diplomacy.
It is safety.

Any individual whose behavior introduces physical risk, emotional instability, or unpredictable aggression is not permitted aboard, regardless of past friendship, shared history, or social status.

There is no “joking” with violence.
There is no “playful” harm.
There is no “edgy humor” that endangers the body or the mind.

If a person has ever treated your safety as entertainment, they have disqualified themselves from proximity to the crew.

---

II. THE DIFFERENCE BETWEEN A FRIEND AND A PERFORMER

Future captains must learn this early:

Some people present a polished version of themselves in the spotlight.
But the truth of a person is revealed when the spotlight turns off.

A friend remains consistent.
A performer collapses into chaos, manipulation, or dominance games.

Discernment Rule:
When someone’s “off‑stage” behavior contradicts their “on‑stage” persona, trust the off‑stage data. That is the real person.

---

III. THE RED FLAGS THAT REQUIRE IMMEDIATE DISTANCE

A captain must withdraw access when an individual demonstrates any of the following:

• Physical boundary violations, even once
• Attempts at intimidation disguised as humor
• Identity instability used to avoid accountability
• Resource extraction without reciprocity
• Spotlight‑seeking behavior that disrupts crew cohesion
• Past betrayal followed by inconsistent behavior


These are not personality quirks.
These are destabilizing threat vectors.

---

IV. NON‑ESCALATION PROTOCOL

When removing an unsafe former ally from your orbit:

1. Do not argue.
2. Do not justify.
3. Do not negotiate.
4. Do not re‑enter the emotional arena.


You simply close the airlock.

Block where necessary.
Restrict where appropriate.
Maintain ceremonial respect on shared channels.
Move forward without commentary.

This is not punishment.
This is hull maintenance.

---

V. THE CAPTAIN’S FINAL WORD

A vessel is not a public venue.
It is a sanctuary for the crew, the children, and the mission.

If someone’s presence introduces fear, instability, or danger — even if they claim to be “reformed” — the captain is obligated to deny them entry.

A captain does not gamble with safety.
A captain does not hope someone has changed.
A captain acts on the data.

Your “no” is not personal.
Your “no” is stewardship.
Your “no” protects the next generation.
