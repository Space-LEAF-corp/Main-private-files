SPACE LEAF Emotional Safety Standard 1.0

Humane Stability Protocols for Deep‑Space and High‑Stress Environments

Issued by: Space LEAF Corp
Version: 1.0
Status: Active Standard
Applies to: Cadets, Crew, Children, Remote Participants, Mission Support Personnel
Supersedes: None (initial release)

---

1. Purpose

The SPACE LEAF Emotional Safety Standard establishes the required emotional, cognitive, and psychological protections for all individuals participating in Space LEAF missions, simulations, remote exploration, or training programs.
This standard ensures that no human—especially no child—is ever left emotionally unsupported in environments where isolation, unfamiliar sensory conditions, and mission constraints can induce panic or destabilization.

---

2. Guiding Principles

• Human‑First Engineering: Systems must be designed around real human reactions, not idealized astronaut behavior.
• Emotion as Valid Signal: Emotional responses are treated as actionable data, not faults.
• Grounding in Alien Environments: Every environment must provide psychological anchors when gravity, ground, and escape are absent.
• Continuous Support: Emotional safety is an ongoing mission layer, not a one‑time training event.
• Non‑Punitive Response: Systems must never shame, blame, or punish emotional reactions.
• Child‑Safe by Default: All emotional safety mechanisms assume children may be present or participating remotely.


---

3. Architecture Overview

The Emotional Safety Architecture consists of five integrated layers:

1. Human State Sensing Layer
2. Grounding & Stabilization Layer
3. Communication & Support Layer
4. Interface & Interaction Safety Layer
5. Training & Simulation Layer


Each layer is mandatory for all Space LEAF systems and missions.

---

4. Human State Sensing Layer

4.1 Baseline Emotional Profiling

Systems must establish a safe, non‑intrusive emotional baseline for each participant during training.

4.2 Real‑Time Stress Indicators

Systems must monitor interaction patterns that indicate distress, including:

• Hesitation
• Repeated cancels
• Error bursts
• Emergency button hovering
• Freeze or inactivity during critical phases


4.3 Contextual Awareness

Emotional interpretation must consider:

• Mission phase
• Isolation level
• Duration
• Environmental stressors
• Age and developmental stage (for children)


---

5. Grounding & Stabilization Layer

5.1 Sensory Grounding Cues

Systems must provide grounding anchors such as:

• Familiar visual cues (Earth horizon, soft lighting)
• Gentle auditory cues (heartbeat‑like rhythms)
• Haptic reassurance (subtle vibration patterns)


5.2 Guided Regulation Sequences

When stress is detected, systems must initiate structured calming protocols including:

• Breathing guidance
• Micro‑checklists
• “You are safe; here’s what’s happening” scripts


5.3 Safe Pause Mode

Non‑critical tasks must pause automatically when emotional distress is detected.
UI must simplify and guide the user through grounding without judgment.

---

6. Communication & Support Layer

6.1 Immediate Human Contact

Participants must have priority access to mission support personnel during emotional distress.

6.2 Companion Presence Systems

Systems must provide reassuring presence through:

• Mission support avatars
• Familiar voices
• Guardian/parent messages for children


6.3 Structured Emotional Check‑Ins

Long missions must include scheduled emotional health check‑ins logged as part of mission telemetry.

---

7. Interface & Interaction Safety Layer

7.1 Panic‑Proof UI

Critical controls (airlocks, suits, life support) must include multi‑step confirmations designed to slow impulsive actions.

7.2 Humane Messaging

System language must remain calm, clear, and non‑alarmist—even during emergencies.

7.3 Gentle Error Recovery

Errors must trigger supportive guidance:
“Let’s fix this together. Here’s the next step.”

---

8. Training & Simulation Layer

8.1 Emotional Drills

Simulations must include emotional scenarios such as:

• Panic
• Isolation
• Disorientation
• “I want to go home” moments


8.2 Pre‑Mission Psychological Briefing

Cadets must be taught:
“You cannot step outside or go home until the mission ends. Here’s how we stay grounded.”

8.3 Post‑Scenario Reflection

Every intense simulation must end with structured reflection on emotional responses and coping tools.

---

9. High‑Risk Emotional State Protocols

9.1 Trigger Detection

Defined patterns must activate emotional safety protocols.

9.2 Tiered Response Ladder

Tier 1: UI calming + grounding cues
Tier 2: Guided regulation sequence
Tier 3: Live human contact
Tier 4: Temporary reassignment of critical controls (with consent)

9.3 No Isolation Rule

No participant may face emotional distress alone.
Presence—human or digital—is mandatory.

---

10. Child‑Specific Emotional Safety

10.1 Age‑Appropriate Explanations

Systems must explain events in simple, non‑frightening language.

10.2 Guardian Presence

Children must have access to guardian/parent presence through ICU glasses or remote channels.

10.3 Play‑Based Grounding

Games, stories, and familiar characters may be used as emotional anchors.

---

11. Governance & Accountability

11.1 Mandatory Compliance

Emotional safety is a required mission system, not optional.

11.2 Emotional Safety Audits

Regular audits must review logs, incidents, and feedback.

11.3 Stewardship Board Oversight

A dedicated board must ensure emotional safety mechanisms respect dignity, privacy, and autonomy.

---

12. Standard Maintenance

This standard must be reviewed annually and updated based on:

• Mission data
• Cadet feedback
• Psychological research
• Technological advancements
• Ethical review findings


---

13. Certification

Systems, training programs, and mission protocols must pass the SPACE LEAF Emotional Safety Compliance Check before deployment.

---

14. Closing Statement

Space LEAF Corp affirms that emotional safety is life support.
In environments where gravity, ground, and escape do not exist, emotional stability becomes the anchor that keeps humanity intact.

This standard ensures that every participant—child or adult—remains grounded, supported, and never alone.
